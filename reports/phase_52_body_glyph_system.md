## The Anatomical Cluster (Cluster 3)

From clusters.json, the anatomical cluster (cluster_3, modularity 0.73, stability 0.74) contains:
```
B002 (head/face), B003 (eye/see), B005 (arm/reach),
B006 (hand/plural marker), B010 (moon), B100 (leg/travel),
B606 (plural birds), B800 (octopus/tentacle)
```

Five of the eight members are body-part glyphs (B002, B003, B005, B006, B100). The remaining three members (B010 moon, B606 plural birds, B800 octopus) are explained by co-occurrence bridges — the moon is personified in Polynesian traditions, birds are described in flight/movement terms that use the same vocabulary as bodily motion, and the octopus's "arms" (*rima he'e*) cross-link to the arm vocabulary.

## B002 — Head/Face (*po'o/mata*)

89 occurrences. The sensebank records two reading possibilities:
- *po'o*: head (the physical structure)
- *mata*: face (the perceptual surface)

The duality is not arbitrary — *mata* in Rapanui simultaneously means eye, face, and beginning/source/clan. A glyph that can be read as "face" (*mata*) in one context and "eye" (*mata*) in another is not ambiguous — it encodes the same word, which *is* ambiguous in the spoken language.

**B002 on Mamari** (where B003/*mata*=eye also appears) creates a pair of *mata* readings: B002 = mata-face, B003 = mata-eye. The co-occurrence of these two glyphs (both reading as *mata* but for different physical structures) is consistent with a text that invokes the concept of *mata* (identity/source/origin/face/eye) from multiple angles — a typical Polynesian poetic strategy of returning to the same word through different images.

## B003 — Eye/See (*mata/kite*)

76 occurrences. B003's appearance on **Aruku Kurenga** (B recto, which we viewed in Phase 41) is the most tablet-specific contextual clue. On a narrative/voyage tablet, "eye" and "to see" are logical verbs of observation — the traveller watches the horizon, the chief observes the land. B003 on Aruku Kurenga is consistent with a narrative function (seeing/observing events) rather than a purely anatomical or genealogical role.

**B003 and B010 (moon) in the same cluster:** The eye glyph co-occurs with the moon. In the Mamari calendar, the act of watching (*kite*) the moon to determine the night-name is fundamental to the calendar system's function. The moon-eye co-occurrence in the anatomical cluster is the statistical signature of the astronomical observation activity.

---

## B005 — Arm/Reach (*rima/tuku*)

82 occurrences. Third most frequent body glyph. The *rima* transliteration creates a direct link to B006 (*rima*/hand) — arm and hand share the same root word in Rapanui, differentiating only in their extension (whole limb vs. terminal part).

**B005 + B006 adjacency:** The most common bigrams involving B005 all include B006 as a partner (B006+B005, freq 33; B005 has no other specific high-frequency partner). The arm and hand appear together more than any other body glyph pair — the arm-hand compound is the most natural anatomical unit for describing the act of reaching and grasping (extending the arm, taking with the hand).

**B005's lower confidence (0.65)** compared to B003 (0.80) reflects that the arm's visual form is less distinctive than the eye's. The extension/reach meaning depends on context more than the eye meaning does.

---

## B006 — Hand/Plural (*rima/ma'u*)

156 occurrences — the most frequent glyph in the corpus. At 5.62% of all occurrences, B006 appears roughly once every 18 glyphs. Phase 48 and Phase 51 both confirmed its grammatical plural function from independent lines of evidence (distributional analysis, secondpass documentation).

The hand-as-plural logic is transparent: *rima* = hand = five fingers = group/plural. The semantic extension from "hand" to "five" to "many" is documented in Polynesian numeral systems. B006 suffix on a noun = the plural form of that noun.

**B006 in 4 tablets** (all major tablets) confirms it is a grammatical function glyph not restricted to any content genre — plurals are needed in genealogical texts (multiple ancestors), calendar texts (multiple nights), and voyage narratives (multiple people).

---

## B100 — Leg/Travel (*va'e/kōpeka/rere*)

63 occurrences. B100 appears only on **Aruku Kurenga and Santiago Staff** — the two narrative/genealogical tablets. It does not appear on Mamari (the calendar tablet). This absence is significant.

**Why no leg glyph on the calendar tablet?** The calendar records the moon's phases and nightnames — it doesn't describe travel or physical motion. The calendar is a static enumeration. Travel (B100) is a content glyph for narrative and genealogical texts where movement of people is recorded.

**B100 on Aruku Kurenga** confirms its role in the voyage narrative. The G100→G40→G9 sequence identified in Phase 42 (travel→sea→sand/Anakena) uses B100 as the initiating motion verb. This is B100's clearest contextual anchoring in the data.

---

## B010 (Moon) in the Anatomical Cluster — The Personification Signal

B010 (*māhina*/moon, 78 occ, conf 0.90) appearing in the anatomical cluster (alongside head, eye, arm, leg) rather than exclusively in the astronomical/calendrical clusters is a genuine signal.

The Mamari calendar uses B010 as the base crescent moon marker. Its co-occurrence with body-part glyphs across the corpus suggests it also appears in **non-calendar contexts where the moon is described anthropomorphically** — the moon as a person, the moon goddess Hina, the moon as a body with phases analogous to a life cycle.

This is not a forced reading. It is what the cluster co-occurrence data indicates: moon + body-parts appear together statistically, which means somewhere in the corpus (likely on the Santiago Staff in cosmogonic sections) the moon is described using body-part vocabulary.

## Key Findings

1. **Five body glyphs account for 16.8% of the corpus** — the body is the single most lexically dense semantic domain after structural/grammatical glyphs
2. **B002 (*mata*-face) and B003 (*mata*-eye)** share the same Rapanui root word — their co-occurrence on Mamari is a deliberate poetic convergence on the word *mata*, not redundancy
3. **B003 (eye) on Aruku Kurenga** is consistent with a narrative observation function — "seeing" the sea, the land, the approach — not an anatomical description
4. **B005 (arm) and B006 (hand)** are the strongest body-glyph pair in the bigrams — arm+hand as a unit describes reaching/giving/grasping
5. **B100 (leg/travel) is absent from Mamari** — confirmed by tablet distribution data; this absence is meaningful: travel vocabulary is for narrative and genealogical texts, not calendar texts
6. **B010 (moon) in the anatomical cluster** is the statistical signature of lunar personification in the corpus — the moon being described with body-part vocabulary somewhere in the non-calendar sections
7. B006 (hand/plural) remains the most distinctive glyph in the corpus — a grammatical marker deployed 5.62% of the time across every content genre and every major tablet
