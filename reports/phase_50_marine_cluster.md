## The Four Turtle/Marine Reptile Glyphs

Having four distinct turtle-related glyphs is the marine cluster's most unusual feature. The Rapanui relationship with turtles was significant — turtles (*honu*) were sacred, associated with chiefs, with the moon, and with deep ocean navigation.

### G280 — The Bridge Glyph (*honu*, conf 0.95)
- 10 occurrences, Mamari, astronomical AND calendrical AND marine clusters
- Highest-confidence marine creature identification
- **Calendar role confirmed:** dark moon position on Mamari; mid-month position on Keiti (Phase 45)
- **Petroglyph confirmation:** Rapa Nui petroglyph shows turtles dividing crescent sequences
- The G280 bridge between marine and calendrical clusters is genuine — the turtle's dual role (sacred marine creature AND moon-phase marker) is confirmed by three independent sources

### G068 — Deep Water Creature (*hohonu ika/honu*, conf 0.90)
- 41 occurrences, Mamari and Santiago Staff
- "Turtle pictograph associated with god Rongo and used as name/epithet in genealogies"
- The "deep water" specification distinguishes G068 from G280 — this is not the beach/shore turtle but the open-ocean deep-water turtle, associated with **Rongo** (the agricultural/moon god in Rapa Nui)
- Appearing in genealogies as a name/epithet means G068 may encode a chief's name or title incorporating *honu* (turtle) — a common Polynesian chiefly name element

### G750 — Ancient Turtle (*honu/mārō*, conf 0.70)
- 33 occurrences, Mamari and Santiago Staff
- "Very old or from distant past; marine reptile with temporal qualities: 'turtle' (honu) + 'ancient' (tawhito)"
- The "ancient/slow" quality of the turtle — one of Earth's most long-lived animals — is the semantic extension here
- Where G280 marks the moon's cycle and G068 marks deep-water association with Rongo, **G750 marks temporal depth** — the very old, the ancestral past, things that persist across generations

### G068 vs G280 vs G750: Three Turtles, Three Domains
- G280 = moon/calendar (time cycles in the sky)
- G068 = deep ocean/Rongo (sacred marine space)
- G750 = antiquity/ancestry (time depth on earth)

The script differentiated three culturally significant aspects of *honu* (turtle) into three distinct glyphs. This is not redundancy — it is precision.

## G700 — The Fish/Victim Duality (*ika/kai*, conf 0.95)

G700 is the highest-frequency marine glyph:
- 45 occurrences, Santiago Staff
- **Two confirmed meanings: fish (ika) AND victim/war casualty**

This duality is documented in the secondpass (phase6.1.md):
> *"Glyph 700, which depicts a fish, carries meanings 'fish' (ika) or 'victim/sacrifice'. If a chief is shown with a fish glyph, it could imply 'Chief [So-and-so], the fisherman' or 'the provider', or perhaps allude to a legend where that chief procured a great catch (since ika can metaphorically mean the 'victim' or prize of a competition)."*

The *ika* / victim duality exists in Polynesian languages: *ika* = fish (universal Polynesian), but *ika* also = "catch" (the thing caught), and in martial contexts = the "catch" of war, i.e., the fallen enemy. The same word covers the fish in the ocean and the enemy struck down in battle.

On the Santiago Staff, G700's 45 occurrences are almost all in the genealogical/chiefly context where the chief (G200) is associated with G700. This is consistent with the secondpass reading of line a3 type structures across multiple tablets — but whether G700 means literal fish (provisions, tribute) or metaphorical victory-catch in each instance requires context-specific analysis that cannot be confirmed at this stage.

## G730 — Shark/Tangaroa (*mako/Tangaroa*, conf 0.65)

- 16 occurrences, Santiago Staff
- "Stylized shark representing sea god Tangaroa or big predator; used in genealogies"
- Tangaroa is the pan-Polynesian sea deity — in Rapa Nui, Makemake largely replaced Tangaroa as the primary creator deity, but Tangaroa's marine domain persisted
- G730 in the ritual cluster (cluster_8) alongside G600 (frigatebird/Makemake) and G069 (lizard/Hiro) suggests G730 appears in the context of deity-naming or divine genealogy — not in fishing-catalog contexts

**The Tangaroa connection:** Secondpass-phase13.md notes "a fish glyph appearing alongside a sun glyph alludes to the myth of Makemake." Similarly, G730 alongside G200 (chief) in a genealogical context may be encoding a chief's divine ancestry through Tangaroa. Polynesian chiefs routinely claimed descent from specific deities, and Tangaroa lineages are documented in multiple island traditions.

## G800 — Octopus/Tentacle (*he'e/rima he'e*, conf 0.55)

- 19 occurrences, Santiago Staff only
- "Octopus or tentacle glyph; eight-armed marine creature"
- Lower confidence (0.55) but confirmed on Santiago Staff in marine context
- The *rima he'e* (octopus arm/rima = hand/arm) connection noted in Phase 46's anatomical cluster analysis means G800 bridges the marine and anatomical domains through the "arm" vocabulary

Octopuses (*he'e*) in Hawaiian and other Polynesian traditions are associated with the primordial sea, with shapeshifting, and with the god Kanaloa/Tangaroa. If G800 on the Santiago Staff appears near G730 (Tangaroa), the compound may encode a specific myth about Tangaroa's octopus manifestation.

---

## G710 — Predatory Fish/Danger (*mako/taratara*, conf 0.65)

- 24 occurrences, Santiago Staff and Small Santiago
- "Aggressive or violent in nature; fish with fierce teeth; denotes shark or predatory fish, ferocious quality"
- The "danger" (*kino*) meaning alongside "sharp-toothed predator" suggests G710 encodes a **moral/social quality** through the predator metaphor — not just a specific fish species but the quality of being dangerous, aggressive, sharp

G710's appearance on both the Santiago Staff AND Small Santiago (not just the Staff) distinguishes it from G730 (Staff only) and G800 (Staff only). G710's social/moral reading is more broadly applicable than the specific Tangaroa deity association of G730.

## The Sea Itself: G040 in the Marine Cluster

G040 (*vai/tai/sea*, conf 0.80, 67 occurrences) — the wavy-line sea glyph — appears in three clusters: marine (physical water), astronomical (phase 45 crescent variant reading), and calendrical. As the medium in which all marine creatures exist, G040's membership in the marine cluster is unsurprising. But its simultaneous presence in the astronomical and calendrical clusters reinforces the Polynesian identification of sea-patterns with sky-patterns — the same vocabulary governs both ocean and heavens.

## Tablet Distribution — Why the Marine Cluster Is Santiago Staff-Dominant

Of the 11 marine cluster members, the majority are attested primarily or exclusively on the Santiago Staff:
- G700 (45 occ): Santiago Staff
- G730 (16 occ): Santiago Staff
- G800 (19 occ): Santiago Staff
- G710 (24 occ): Santiago Staff, Small Santiago
- G750 (33 occ): Mamari, Santiago Staff

This concentration on the Santiago Staff is meaningful given the Staff's confirmed genealogical content. Marine creatures appearing in a genealogical/royal context are not a fishing catalog — they are most likely:
1. **Chiefly epithets** — a chief named for a specific fish (e.g., "Shark-chief," "Deep-water-chief")
2. **Divine ancestry markers** — Tangaroa-derived lineages using G730
3. **Ritual sacrifice records** — G700's victim/ika meaning in a warfare/tribute context

The marine cluster in a genealogical text = the intersection of the ocean domain with human social structures.

## Key Findings

1. **Four distinct turtle glyphs** (G280, G068, G750 and partially G730) encode different aspects of *honu* (turtle): moon-calendar, deep-ocean/Rongo, temporal depth/antiquity
2. **G280 bridges marine and calendrical domains** — its dark-moon position on Mamari and mid-month position on Keiti, confirmed also by a Rapa Nui petroglyph, makes this the corpus's best cross-domain bridge glyph
3. **G700's fish/victim duality** (*ika* = fish AND war-catch) is a genuine lexical ambiguity in Rapanui that the script inherits — both meanings are active in different contexts on the Santiago Staff
4. **G730 (shark/Tangaroa)** appears in the ritual cluster as well as marine — in genealogical contexts it likely encodes divine ancestry through Tangaroa rather than a literal shark
5. **The marine cluster is Santiago Staff-dominant** — marine creatures in the Staff's context are chiefly epithets, divine ancestry markers, or ritual references, not a fishing catalog
6. **G040 (sea/water)** in marine + astronomical + calendrical clusters confirms the Polynesian ocean-sky vocabulary convergence
7. The marine cluster's 11-member size (second only to astronomical) reflects the centrality of the ocean to Rapa Nui life — but its composition (multiple deity-associated creatures) suggests the marine domain in the script is primarily **symbolic and genealogical** rather than ecological
