**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Status:** Complete (Evidence-anchored)

## The Glyph's Visual Form

G67 depicts a branching, tree-like structure. The species identified is *Paschalococos disperta* — the **extinct Easter Island palm** that once covered the island before deforestation. This palm was central to Rapa Nui ecology: it provided food, fibre, wood for canoes and rollers to move moai, and shade. Its disappearance is tied to the island's environmental collapse. The glyph preserves the memory of a tree that no longer exists when the tablets were almost certainly carved.

---

## The Three Meaning Clusters

### Cluster A: Literal Tree — Extinct Palm (*niu / Paschalococos*)
The primary pictographic referent. *Niu* is the Polynesian word for coconut palm (closest living relative); *pae* may refer to the palm's structural form. In practical tablet contexts (lists of resources, agricultural inventories), G67 = "the palm tree."

### Cluster B: Cycle / Repeat
A palm tree embodies cyclical growth — fronds extend, die, regrow. Seasonally it fruits, then rests. In calendar/temporal contexts, this cycle meaning activates: G67 marks **a repeating unit** — a cycle of the moon, a season, or a recurring ritual period.

The lexicon explicitly lists "cycle (repeat)" and "vegetation cycle" as meanings. The context tag `cyclical` is present.

### Cluster C: **Waning Half / Post-Full-Moon Phase Marker**
This is the most calendar-specific meaning and the most significant new finding from the lexicon for Phase 27.

The lexicon explicitly lists:
> **"post-full-moon phase marker"**  
> **"waning half"**

This means G67 is attested as marking the **second half of the lunar month** on the Mamari tablet — the waning phase that runs from night 16 (Maure, after B152 full moon) through to the dark moon (B280). On Mamari, G67 likely appears as a **structural label** for the waning half, functioning like a section header saying: "the waning cycle begins here."

---

## Why a Palm Tree Marks the Waning Moon

The cultural logic is coherent:

1. **The palm loses its fronds** in the dry season — it "wanes" visually, shedding material, just as the moon wanes in light
2. **The palm's cycle** (flourish → shed → renew) mirrors the lunar cycle (wax → full → wane → new)
3. The transliteration includes **"Rongo"** — the god of agriculture and (in some traditions) the waning moon. Rongo governs the waning phase, the period of gathering-in rather than growing-out
4. In Māori and other Polynesian traditions, the waning moon phase is associated with **agricultural work** — harvesting, storing — which is Rongo's domain

So G67 carries this chain: *palm tree → seasonal shedding → waning → Rongo's season → waning moon phase*. The scribe did not have to explain this — any trained reciter would have understood that the palm-tree glyph signals "we are now in the waning half."

## G67 on the Santiago Staff (29 occurrences spanning Mamari + Staff)

On the Santiago Staff, G67 likely activates its **cycle/creation** meaning rather than the specific waning-phase calendar meaning. In a cosmogonic genealogy, the extinct palm might represent:

- The primordial vegetation of the island — "the palm was here from the beginning"
- A cycle marker in the creation sequence — "one cycle completes, another begins"
- A Rongo reference in a list of divine forces

The 29 total occurrences across both tablets suggests G67 is a **medium-frequency glyph** with distinct but related roles in calendrical vs. cosmogonic contexts.

---

## G67 and Environmental Memory

There is a poignant dimension to G67 that cannot be overlooked. The extinct palm (*Paschalococos disperta*) was almost certainly already disappearing when many of the surviving tablets were carved (estimated 13th–17th century CE). By the time European contact occurred in 1722, the forest was largely gone.

**G67 is thus a preserved memory of a vanished tree.** The Rapanui scribes encoded the palm in their script at a time when they may have already been watching it disappear. This makes G67 not just a calendar marker but a cultural record — the script itself is evidence that the palm was important enough to be immortalised in writing even as it was lost to deforestation.

---

## Key Findings

1. **G67's "post-full-moon phase marker" and "waning half" meanings** are explicitly listed in the lexicon — this is a direct calendar role, not an inference.
2. The palm-tree/waning-moon connection is culturally motivated through Rongo (god of waning moon and agriculture) and the palm's seasonal cycle.
3. G67 on **Mamari**: waning-phase marker (second half of the lunar month).
4. G67 on **Santiago Staff**: cycle/creation meaning (Rongo reference or primordial vegetation).
5. The glyph is an **environmental memory** — the extinct *Paschalococos* preserved in carved wood even as the real trees were disappearing.
6. Translit includes "Rongo" — the deity whose name is also the script's own name — making G67 a theophoric reference as well as a botanical one.
