## What Remains Genuinely Uncertain

### The Phonetic Layer
We can read Rongorongo **semantically** — we know what many glyphs mean. We cannot yet read it **phonetically** — we cannot reliably produce the spoken Rapanui words a 17th-century reciter would have said for most sequences. The polysemic logographic nature of the script means a phonetic key (a syllabary or consistent phoneme-to-shape mapping) has not been confirmed.

The transliterations in the lexicon (*tangata, ariki, mahina, moko*…) are candidate words, not confirmed phonetic readings. They represent what *should* have been said based on cultural context — they may be correct, but they are not proven.

### Tablets H, P, Q (The "Grand Tradition")
These three tablets are confirmed to share parallel passages with the Santiago Staff. But the master lexicon does not list specific glyphs for them in `tablets_found`. This means our confirmed-glyph methodology cannot be applied to H, P, Q without additional attestation data.

### ~19 of 30 Mamari Calendar Nights
Only 7 positions in the 30-night calendar are confirmed at ≥0.80 confidence. The remaining ~19 nights have candidate glyphs but lack the multiple independent evidence lines needed for confidence.

### Personal Names
No individual personal name (chief, ancestor) has been confidently identified with a specific sound sequence. The structure of names in lineage chains (the "name cluster" positions) is understood, but the glyphs within those positions cannot be read aloud.

### G380, G73, G15, G17, G33, G45, G46, G72
These glyphs remain either unidentified (G380: awaiting decipherment), very low-occurrence (1–2 times), or low-confidence (≤0.50). They require more attestation.

## The Honest Bottom Line

Rongorongo is not fully deciphered. What it is: **semantically partially readable** — we understand what domains of content each tablet encodes, we can identify the structural scaffold, and we can confirm specific high-confidence passages (notably the full moon, the creation triplet, the Anakena landfall). What we cannot yet do: read a complete new passage aloud in Rapanui with confidence.

The script encodes a rich, internally consistent, culturally grounded system. The more we confirm, the more coherent it becomes. The polysemy is not a problem — it is the design. Rongorongo was made to be read by people who already carried the cultural context in their heads. The glyphs are mnemonics that unlock chants already known. Our task is to reconstruct enough of that contextual knowledge that the glyphs can unlock their texts again.

That reconstruction is well advanced. It is not complete.
