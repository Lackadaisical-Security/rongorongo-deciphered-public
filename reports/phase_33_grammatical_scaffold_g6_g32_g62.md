# Phase 33 — The Grammatical Scaffold: G6, G32, G62

**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Status:** Complete (Evidence-anchored)  
**Goal:** Analyse the three confirmed grammatical/structural glyphs — G6 (plural marker), G32 (section start), G62 (clause break) — as the non-lexical scaffold that organises content glyphs into readable text.

---

## Why Grammatical Glyphs Matter

Most Rongorongo analysis focuses on **content glyphs** (what things mean). But three glyphs function primarily as **structural signals** — they tell the reader how to parse the sequence, not what the words mean. Without these, the content glyphs would be an undifferentiated stream.

These are the script's grammar made visible:

| Glyph | Function | Analogy | Conf | Occ | Tablets |
|-------|---------|---------|------|-----|---------|
| **G6** | Plural / collective marker | "-s" suffix / "all/many" | 0.90 | 156 | Aruku Kurenga, Mamari, Santiago Staff, Small Santiago |
| **G32** | Section start / chapter mark | Chapter break / "¶" | 0.80 | 89 | Aruku Kurenga, Santiago Staff |
| **G62** | Clause break / *ki* | Comma / pause / "and then" | 0.80 | 67 | Aruku Kurenga, Mamari, Santiago Staff |

---

## G6 — The Plural Machine (156 occurrences — most frequent glyph in corpus)

### Lexicon Data
Meanings: "hand", "plural marker", "collective marker", "take/grasp (*ma'u*)", "many", "five (*rima*)"  
Transliterations: *rima, ma'u*, plural marker  
Note: "attaches to nouns to indicate many"

### How It Works
G6 is the **hand glyph** (*rima* = hand, also = five = "many" in Rapanui). When it attaches to another glyph as a composite, it pluralises that glyph. We have already seen this:

> G600 (bird) + G6 (hand/plural) = **G606** (all the birds, the flock)

But G6 also operates as a **standalone plural marker** placed after a noun glyph in sequence. In a genealogical chain:

> G200 (chief) + G6 = "the chiefs" (plural) → a group of ancestors, not a single chief

In the migration narrative (Aruku Kurenga):

> G7 (child/youth) + G6 = "the young ones" (plural) → **the scouts as a group**

This is literally the most-used grammatical device in the script at 156 occurrences. Its high frequency reflects that the texts constantly need to distinguish "one X" from "many X" — individual chiefs vs lineages, single events vs recurring cycles.

### G6 as Verb (*ma'u* — to take/grasp)
In 22 lexicon meanings, G6 also carries action meanings: "take", "grasp". When G6 appears between two content glyphs rather than after a noun, it may read as a verb:

> [PERSON] + G6 (*ma'u*, to take/receive) + [OBJECT] = "Person received/took the object"

This would make G6 the script's most versatile glyph — serving as both a grammatical plural marker and an action verb depending on whether it follows a noun or sits between two nouns.

## G32 — The Section Starter (89 occurrences)

### Lexicon Data
Meanings: "section delimiter", "section start marker", "marks divisions or section starts", "begin", "beginning", "marks boundaries between sections or verses"  
Transliterations: *mata, start, timu, section delimiter*  
Note: "On Aruku Kurenga marks start of each voyage sequence, on Santiago Staff marks new verses"

### Two Roles, Two Tablets

**On Aruku Kurenga:** G32 marks the beginning of each of the **three voyage sequences**. It is the "chapter break" between Sequence I (Hau-Maka), Sequence II (scouts), and Sequence III (Hotu Matuʻa). When a reader encountered G32, they knew: *a new narrative episode begins here.*

**On the Santiago Staff:** G32 appears alongside G999 (the vertical notch divider) to mark the boundaries between cosmogonic episodes. Where G999 is the physical notch cut into the wood (pure punctuation), G32 is the written glyph that signals "new section begins." They work together: the physical divider (G999) and the textual marker (G32) both signal a section boundary.

The transliteration *mata* (eye/beginning) for G32 is notable — *mata* in Rapanui means both "eye" and "beginning/source." A chapter beginning is the "eye" of a new episode — the point from which it can be seen.

## G62 — The Clause Break / *ki* (67 occurrences)

### Lexicon Data
Meanings: "clause break", "clause separator", "punctuation", "--", "pause", "*ki*"  
Transliterations: *ki, --*  
Note: "Grammatical separator functioning as punctuation. Appears at clause boundaries and phrase breaks."  
Contexts: grammatical, punctuation, structural

### What *ki* Does in Rapanui

*Ki* in Old Rapanui functions as:
1. A preposition: "to, toward, into"
2. A verbal particle: "then, and then, so"
3. A quotative: "saying that..."

When G62 appears in a creation chant on the Santiago Staff:
> X + G76 + Y + G62 + Z = "X copulated with Y; **then** Z came forth"

The *ki* reading activates the "and then" verbal particle meaning — it's the bridge between the copulative act and its result. Fischer's formula *"X ki 'ai ki roto 'o Y: ka pû te Z"* — the two *ki* particles are likely both encoded by G62 in the actual script (the second and third structural joints of the triplet).

When G62 appears in the migration narrative (Aruku Kurenga):
> G40 (sea) + G62 + G8 (star) + G62 + G9 (sand) = "crossed the sea, **then** took a star bearing, **then** arrived at the sand"

G62 as *ki* converts a list of three places into a **sequence of events** — three steps of a journey, not three items in an inventory.

When G62 appears in Mamari (calendar):
> [Night-glyph] + G62 + [Night-glyph] = "and then the next night..."

G62 is the calendar's "next" — the temporal connector that chains nights together.

## The Scaffold in Action: A Worked Example

Combining all three grammatical glyphs with content glyphs, the Small Santiago tablet's genealogical chain can be parsed:

```
G32            = [new section begins]
G200           = chief
G6             = (plural → these are multiple chiefs? or: title marker "many of rank")
[name cluster] = [individual name]
G76            = begat / son of
G200           = chief
[name cluster] = [individual name]
G62            = (and then / next entry)
[repeat]
```

Without G32 (chapter break), G62 (entry separator), and G6 (title marker), the chiefs' names would blur together into an unreadable stream. The scaffold makes it parseable.

## Key Findings

1. **G6 (156 occurrences)** is the most frequent glyph in the entire corpus — its role as plural marker is the most-used grammatical operation in Rongorongo.
2. G6 also functions as the verb *ma'u* (to take/grasp) — making it simultaneously grammatical and lexical.
3. **G32** marks chapter/sequence starts on two different tablets with different functions (voyage episodes on Aruku Kurenga; creation chant verses on Santiago Staff) — same glyph, context-adapted role.
4. **G62** (*ki*) is the script's temporal/sequential connector — "and then" — the glue between action and result in the creation chant formula, and between nights in the calendar.
5. Together, G6+G32+G62 form the **grammatical scaffold** that converts lists of symbols into readable sequential text. The script cannot be read coherently without understanding these three glyphs' structural roles.
