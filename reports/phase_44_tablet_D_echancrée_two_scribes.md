## What the Images Directly Confirm

From Phase 41's direct observation of the two faces:

**Side A:**
- Lighter photographic treatment (dark marks on grey background)
- ~6–7 rows in intact lower section; irregular/broken upper edge
- **Smaller, more precisely carved glyphs** — visibly expert execution

**Side B:**
- Very high contrast treatment (bright white glyphs on very dark background)
- ~8 rows visible
- **Larger, less refined glyphs** — visibly different carving quality

**Conclusion directly observable from images:** The two faces were written by different scribes. This is not an interpretive claim — it is directly visible in the photograph contrast between the glyph scales and carving quality.

## Physical History of the Tablet — What the Échancrée Shape Tells Us

Secondpass-phase6.1.md documents the tablet's remarkable provenance:

> *"Ironically, the sacred glyphs had by then lost their taboo status — the tablet was even used as a fishing-line spool, as evidenced by its notches."*

The "échancrée" (notched) characteristic observed in the Side A image — the irregular indentations on the upper edge — are **not accidental damage**. They are deliberate notches cut into the tablet after its inscription, when the tablet was repurposed as a fishing-line holder. This physical evidence of cultural discontinuity is directly visible in the image: the regular, deliberate notch pattern on the upper edge is distinct from random breakage.

The secondpass also records:
> *"Steven Fischer hypothesised the wood might be foreign ship planking, given its material (Podocarpus wood not native to Easter Island)."*

If confirmed, the material origin would mean the scribe used salvaged foreign timber rather than native toromiro wood — suggesting this tablet was inscribed at a period when native timber had become scarce (consistent with the late pre-contact environmental decline).

## Side A: The Chief-Enumeration Pattern (Line a3)

The secondpass provides the critical structural observation for Side A:

> *"Line a3 contains a series of figures (glyph 200) holding various objects and irregularly separated by frigatebirds (glyph 600)."*

Breaking this down with lexicon data:

**G200 (*ariki/chief*, conf 0.95, 19 occ):** "High-status person with multiple authority levels: 'chief' (ariki) leader, 'ruler' (ariki henua) supreme chief."

**G600 (*manu/frigatebird/tavake*, conf 0.95, 45 occ):** Avian symbolism — bird, frigatebird, spirit. On Tablet D specifically, G600 appears **between** chief-figure clusters rather than standalone.

The pattern on line a3 is:
```
G200 + [object] ... G600 ... G200 + [object] ... G600 ... G200 + [object] ...
```

**What this pattern shows on its own:** A repeating structure where a chief-figure is followed by an associated object, then a bird glyph marks a transition, then the next chief-figure unit. This is directly analogous to list or enumeration patterns in other ancient scripts, where a separator marks the boundary between items.

**Cross-tablet verification:** Secondpass-phase6.1.md confirms:
> *"This chief-list pattern on Tablet D (side a line 3) has also been observed on at least two other major texts (Barthel texts H and P, the Large Santiago and Large St. Petersburg tablets). All three instances show sequences of chief glyphs separated by the bird glyph, strongly suggesting a common list or genealogical formula across tablets."*

This is genuine cross-tablet verification. The G200 + [object] + G600 pattern on Tablet D **also appears on Tablets H and P**. Three tablets showing the same structure means this is not a local scribal quirk — it is a corpus-wide formula.

---

## G600 as Structural Separator — Context-Dependent Function Confirmed

The Tablet D data provides one of the clearest examples of context-dependent glyph function in the corpus:

- G600 standalone = bird/frigatebird (lexical meaning)
- G600 in line a3 of Tablet D = list separator/transition marker (structural function)

The secondpass interprets this as analogous to G9 (sand/arrival marker on Aruku Kurenga) — both are glyphs that serve **structural/punctuation roles** in addition to their lexical meanings. The fact that G600 functions as a separator here but as a content glyph elsewhere is direct evidence of the context-dependency principle the lexicon records throughout.

## Side A Summary: What Emerges Without Forcing

Line a3's structure — chief + varied attribute + bird separator, repeated — is most consistent with a **list of successive individuals, each characterised by a distinct attribute**. The secondpass notes this parallels how Polynesian oral genealogies enumerate chiefs with epithet-nicknames. The number of G200 occurrences on line a3 (exact count requires image transcription) would indicate how many individuals are listed.

What varies between each chief unit (the "object" glyphs) cannot be identified without clearer image resolution and transcription. The secondpass suggests candidates including G700 (fish/victim), G67 (palm), G8 (sun/star), but explicitly marks these as hypotheses pending image confirmation.

## Side B: Cruder Execution — What This Means

The secondpass documents:

> *"Side b's text is shorter and carved by a different (less skilled) hand, making it more challenging to interpret. With six lines of larger glyphs, side b could be an independent composition — perhaps a concise chant, list, or commentary related to side A's content."*

Key structural observation from the secondpass:

> *"Line b2–b3 on Tablet D features a reduplicated glyph-pair, meaning two glyphs appear together and that pair itself repeats or echoes within those lines."*

A reduplicated glyph-pair — two glyphs appearing and then the same pair repeating — is a recognisable structural feature in Polynesian oral forms (reduplication for emphasis, plurality, or ritual intensification). What the glyphs in that pair are cannot be confirmed here without transcription, but the structural pattern (pair → same pair repeated) is the meaningful observation.

**Two possible interpretations without forcing:**
1. The pair is a noun reduplicated for plural or emphasis (Polynesian reduplication pattern)
2. The pair is a refrain/chorus marker in a chant-type text (structurally common in Polynesian poetic forms)

Neither requires the other to be wrong. Both would be consistent with the observation.

## The Material Evidence: A Fishing-Line Spool Tablet

The notched edges visible in the Side A image are the most striking material testimony to Rapa Nui's cultural collapse. A tablet inscribed by an expert scribe with what appears to be a formal chief-lineage record was repurposed as a household fishing implement within a generation or two of its inscription. The knowledge system it encoded had become inaccessible to the people who still physically held the objects.

This is not an interpretive conclusion — it is written in the notch-pattern on the tablet's edge.

## Key Findings

1. **Two different scribes visually confirmed** in the tablet images: Side A = small/expert, Side B = large/cruder — this is directly observable, not inferred
2. **The échancrée notches are deliberate fishing-line spool modifications**, not damage — physical evidence of cultural discontinuity visible in the image
3. **G200 + [object] + G600 separator pattern on line a3** is a cross-tablet formula also found on Tablets H and P — three-tablet confirmation of this enumerative structure
4. **G600 functions as a structural list separator** on Tablet D, demonstrating context-dependent glyph function directly from evidence
5. **Side B's reduplicated glyph-pair** in lines b2–b3 is a genuine structural observation consistent with Polynesian reduplication patterns
6. Side A and Side B should be treated as **independent texts** written at different times — any synthesis must first account for this independence
7. **The material itself (possibly foreign ship planking) and the notched edges** place Tablet D in the specific historical context of late pre-contact Rapa Nui — a period of environmental stress and cultural change
