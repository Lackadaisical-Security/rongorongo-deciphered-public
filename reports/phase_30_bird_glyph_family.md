## G600 — The Foundational Bird Glyph

G600 is the **base glyph** for all bird meanings. Its 21 meanings cluster into:

**Literal:** bird (*manu*), frigatebird (*tavake*), fly (*rere*)  
**Spiritual:** soul, spirit, ancestral spirit  
**Cultural:** clan totem, sacred bird, used in creation chants  

The frigatebird (*tavake*) was the most symbolically significant bird on Rapa Nui — its association with the god Makemake made it simultaneously a physical creature and a divine symbol. In any context where G600 appears:

- If near G76 (beget) or G1/G200 (persons): **clan totem** reading — a lineage identified by the frigatebird
- If in a creation chant (G606 version): **spiritual/cosmic** reading — birds as the creative primordial force
- If in a ritual passage: **soul/spirit** reading — the bird as a form the soul takes after death

---

## G606 — The Composite That Drives the Creation Chant

G606 = G600 (bird) + G6 (hand/plural). The lexicon confirms this composite structure explicitly:
> *"Composite of 600 (bird) and 6 (hand); indicates plural birds or a flock."*

But the plural form does more than just multiply birds — the hand (*rima*, which also means "five") adds a collective/multitude sense. G606 thus means **"all the birds"** — every bird, the entire avian order.

This is why the creation chant reads:
> G606 – G76 – G700 – G8 = **"ALL the birds** copulated with the fish; the sun issued forth."

Not "some birds" — ALL birds. The plural/collective meaning of G606 is grammatically essential to the cosmogonic formula. The creative act required the entire category of birds (perhaps representing the sky, the air, the upper world) uniting with the entire category of fish (the sea, the lower world) to produce the sun (light, the mediating force between sky and sea).

This is a **cosmological dualism** encoded in three glyphs.

## G44 — The Ceremony Glyph That Is Also a Frigatebird

G44 presents an interesting case. Its lexicon entry describes it as:
> *"A circular glyph with rhythmic marks, interpreted as 'ceremony' or 'dance'. Likely indicates a ritual event."*

But its meanings also include "frigatebird" and its transliterations are *koro* (ceremony/feast) and *taha* (frigatebird). This glyph sits at the intersection of:

**Bird (literal):** frigatebird → Makemake's bird → the sacred creature whose egg was the prize in the Bird-Man competition  
**Ceremony:** the Bird-Man competition (*Tangata Manu*) **was** a ceremony — the most important annual ritual on Rapa Nui

So G44 may simultaneously mean "frigatebird" AND "the ceremony in which the frigatebird's egg matters." The bird and the rite it generates are encoded in a single glyph. When G44 appears in a text:
- Near personal names/G200: the Bird-Man **competition** — who won, whose clan is identified
- In creation/mythic context: Makemake's sacred **bird** (the cosmic frigatebird)
- In a rhythmic/chant context: a **ceremony/festival** reference

31 occurrences across Santiago Staff and Small Santiago — present enough to be significant in both a genealogical (who won the Bird-Man competition) and cosmogonic (Makemake's role in creation) context.

## G690 — The Bird-Man Hybrid

G690 = a **composite** of a human figure + bird head — literally encoding *tangata manu* (bird-man) in pictographic form. Conf=0.583, 12 occurrences, only on the Santiago Staff.

The secondpass-phase12 describes G690 appearing on Small Santiago Tablet G in a sequence followed by "a verb meaning 'to ascend' and the symbol of an egg" — suggesting a narration of the Bird-Man competition. However, the master lexicon lists G690 only on the **Santiago Staff**, not on Small Santiago — this discrepancy suggests either:
1. G690 appears in both tablets but the lexicon only records one, OR
2. The secondpass description was referring to a different tablet

At 0.583 confidence, G690 should be treated as **tentative but promising**. Its 12 occurrences on the Staff are consistent with the Staff containing references to the Bird-Man ceremony as part of its broader genealogical/cosmogonic scope.

## The Bird Glyph Family as a Semantic Network

```
G600 (single bird / spirit / totem)
    ↓ + G6 (plural/hand)
G606 (ALL birds / collective spiritual force)
    ↑ specific species
G44 (frigatebird / the ceremony the frigatebird makes sacred)
    ↑ + human figure
G690 (bird-man / tangata manu — the human who became the bird)
```

Reading any one of these glyphs activates the network. A scribe writing G690 was invoking G600's soul meaning, G44's ceremonial meaning, and G606's cosmic collective meaning — all simultaneously, in the way that polysemic logographic scripts always work.

## G12 on Mamari — The Calendar's Fish That Flips

A correction from Phase 22: the "fish that flips orientation" in the Mamari calendar is **G12** (conf=0.80, on Mamari + Santiago Staff), not G700. G12 = fish (*ika*) / aquatic motion / "used in divine names such as *Ika roa*." In the calendar context, G12 flipping upside-down after the full moon (B152) encodes the waning phase — the fish swimming the other way. G700 (fish/victim, conf=0.95) appears only on Santiago Staff and Small Santiago — it is the **polysemous sacrifice-fish**, not the Mamari calendar fish.

This distinction matters: Mamari's calendar uses the ordinary fish G12 as a temporal/directional marker; the sacrifice reading of fish belongs to G700 in the Staff's genealogical/war contexts.

## Key Findings

1. **Four distinct bird glyphs** exist in the lexicon — G600 (base), G606 (plural composite), G44 (frigatebird/ceremony), G690 (bird-man hybrid) — each occupying a different semantic niche.
2. **G606 = G600+G6** is a grammatical composite — the plural hand makes it "ALL birds," which is essential to the cosmogonic formula.
3. **G44** encodes both the frigatebird species and the ceremony it makes sacred — a glyph where the sacred animal and the rite it generates are fused.
4. **G690** (bird-man, tangata manu) is tentative (conf=0.583) but has 12 Staff occurrences and is iconographically unambiguous as a composite human+bird figure.
5. **G12 (not G700)** is the fish that flips orientation in the Mamari calendar — a distinction that clarifies both the calendar encoding and the separate sacrifice meaning of G700.
