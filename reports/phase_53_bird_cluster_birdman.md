## B600 — The Primary Frigatebird Glyph (*manu/tavake/rere*)

**45 occurrences** — most frequent bird glyph by far. Tablets: Mamari, Santiago Staff, Small Santiago.

B600 at conf 0.95 (the highest confidence tier) is one of the best-identified glyphs in the corpus. Its three transliterations carry three distinct functions:
- *manu*: bird (generic, the species or the class)
- *tavake*: frigatebird (the specific species, *Fregata minor*)  
- *rere*: to fly, to journey

On Mamari (the calendar tablet), B600 appears in what context? Not as a structural separator — Mamari doesn't show the chief-list pattern. Its presence on Mamari is most consistent with the *manu* (generic bird) or *rere* (flying/journey) meaning in a context that references bird migration or flight as part of a seasonal/astronomical description.

On the Santiago Staff, B600 serves as the **structural separator** between genealogical entries in the bird enumeration section (P005 from Phase 47), and also appears in B600+B690 (frigatebird + birdman) compounds.

**Phase 44's critical confirmation:** On Tablet D line a3, B600 functions as a list separator — "irregularly separating" chief-figure clusters. This context-dependent structural function is directly documented and cross-tablet verified (also on H and P). B600 = structural separator in chief-list context is one of the clearest functional-glyph identifications in the corpus.

## B044 — Ceremony/Frigatebird (*koro/taha*)

**31 occurrences**, Santiago Staff and Small Santiago. Much lower confidence (0.50) than B600.

The lexicon note reveals an unusual glyph situation:

> *"A circular glyph with rhythmic marks, interpreted as 'ceremony' or 'dance'. Likely indicates a ritual event. Found in sequences believed to include ritual activities."*

B044 is described as **circular with rhythmic marks** — this is a very different visual form from the bird-like B600. Yet both share the "frigatebird" identification. The lexicon reconciles this by noting *koro* (ceremony/dance) as the primary meaning alongside *taha* (frigatebird) — the glyph may depict a ceremony associated with the frigatebird cult (the Birdman competition), not the bird itself.

**B044 in both bird and ritual clusters** is consistent with this interpretation: it is the glyph for the **Birdman cult ceremony** (*koro*) rather than the frigatebird as a biological creature. Distinguishing the bird-cult ceremony from the bird itself is exactly the kind of precision that a well-developed script would encode with separate glyphs.

## B606 — Plural Birds (*manu (plural)*, conf 0.95)

**28 occurrences**, Mamari and Santiago Staff. The plural form of B600 — the plural-bird glyph, created (presumably) by some modification of B600's form that signals plurality.

B606's membership in three clusters (human classification, anatomical, bird) reflects the same distributional behaviour as B006 (hand/plural) — plurality markers distribute broadly because they modify many different nouns. B606 may function specifically as "plural birds" (a flock, a migration, multiple bird species in an enumeration) rather than a general plurality marker.

Its appearance in the **human classification cluster** alongside B001 (person), B200 (chief), B300 (woman), and B690 (birdman) suggests B606 appears in sequences that classify humans through their bird-associations — the person who is (or is associated with) multiple birds.

## B690 — The Birdman (*tangata manu*, conf 0.58)

**12 occurrences**, Santiago Staff only. The composite birdman glyph — a half-human, half-bird figure representing the *tangata manu* of the Birdman cult of Orongo.

The Birdman competition was the central political institution of late pre-contact Rapa Nui: each spring, competitors swam to the islet of Motu Nui to retrieve the first sooty tern (*manu tara*) egg and return it to their patron, who would become "Birdman" (*tangata manu*) and hold supreme authority for a year. The winning clan's chief held temporary sovereignty.

B690's 12 occurrences exclusively on the Santiago Staff (the genealogical/chiefly document) places the Birdman specifically in the **genealogical record** — specific chiefs being identified as having been Birdman, or specific lineages being associated with the Birdman cult.

**B690's conf 0.58** (moderate) reflects the composite nature of the glyph — its visual form is clearly human+bird, and its meaning (tangata manu) is well-grounded in cultural knowledge, but the exact semantic function (personal name vs. title vs. ritual marker) in specific occurrences remains uncertain.

## The Birdman-Tangaroa-Makemake Complex

The ritual cluster (cluster_8) brings together:
- B044 (ceremony/frigatebird)
- B069 (lizard/Hiro rain god)
- B600 (frigatebird/Makemake's symbol)
- B700 (fish/victim = sacrifice)
- B730 (shark/Tangaroa)

The ritual cluster's composition is **a deity-complex cluster**: the Birdman cult (B044, B600), the rain deity (B069/Hiro), the sea deity (B730/Tangaroa), and sacrifice/victim (B700). These are the deities invoked in Rapa Nui's major ritual activities.

**What emerges from the cluster without forcing:** In the corpus, these five glyphs appear together in contexts that relate to ritual/ceremonial activity involving multiple deities. A text invoking the Birdman competition might also reference Tangaroa (sea) and Hiro (rain/luck) as part of the same ritual frame.

## Key Findings

1. **B600 (frigatebird/manu, conf 0.95)** is the primary bird glyph — the most frequent (45 occ) and highest-confidence; serves as both content glyph and structural separator depending on context
2. **B600 as list separator on Tablet D (line a3)** is directly confirmed — a prime example of context-dependent glyph function  
3. **B044 (ceremony/koro, conf 0.50)** — not a bird form but a circular ceremony-glyph encoding the Birdman **cult** rather than the bird — confirmed by its "circular with rhythmic marks" visual description and its place in the ritual cluster
4. **B690 (birdman, conf 0.58)** appears exclusively on the Santiago Staff — Birdman cult appearances are specifically in the genealogical/chiefly record, not in calendar or narrative texts
5. **The ritual cluster (B044, B600, B069, B700, B730)** is a deity-complex cluster grouping Birdman cult, rain deity, sea deity, and sacrifice in one co-occurrence space — the ritual activities that brought these deities together encoded together
6. The **bird cluster's low modularity (0.69)** reflects the frigatebird's multiple functional roles: biological creature, deity symbol, structural separator, and ritual marker
