# Phase 41 — Visual Corpus Survey: What the Tablet Images Actually Show

**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Status:** Complete — image-grounded observational record  
**Methodology:** Direct visual observation of 5 tablet faces (B recto, C recto, C verso, D side A, D side B). No interpretations are imposed; only observable structural and physical facts are documented. These observations serve as the cross-tablet verification baseline for all subsequent phases.

---

## The Five Imaged Faces — Observational Record

### Tablet B Recto — Aruku Kurenga
**Physical shape:** Elongated paddle or fish-tail form, clearly wider in the central body and narrowing sharply to a thin handle on one end (the left in the photograph). The shape is immediately distinctive from all other tablets.

**Publication orientation:** The photograph carries a caption "Fig. 157" printed inverted at the top, indicating the source publication reproduced the tablet upside-down — a common occurrence in older scholarly photographic editions. This means the reading begins from the end that appears at the image bottom.

**Inscription density:** Moderate. The rows of glyphs fill the body of the tablet uniformly but glyphs are visually discrete, not crowded against each other. Spacing between individual signs is visible.

**Row count (visible):** Approximately 11 rows are distinguishable across the main body. Row count decreases toward the narrowing handle end as the surface area reduces.

**Boustrophedon:** Clearly visible. Alternate rows show glyphs oriented in opposing directions — glyphs in even-numbered rows appear to be mirrored or rotated relative to odd-numbered rows. This is consistent with the reverse boustrophedon reading documented for all rongorongo texts.

**Glyph categories visually apparent (without identification):**
- Anthropomorphic (upright figure-like) shapes — present in essentially every row
- Curved, animal-like forms — scattered throughout
- Linear/stroke glyphs — interspersed
- Some cross or asterisk-shaped glyphs visible
- No obvious large section-dividing strokes visible (the B999 vertical divider that dominates the Santiago Staff is not apparent here)

**Surface condition:** Generally well-preserved. The wood surface is aged but glyphs are consistently legible. No obvious burn damage.

---

### Tablet C Recto — Mamari
**Physical shape:** Rectangular with gently rounded corners. Substantially larger surface area than Tablet B.

**Photographic technique:** High-contrast rendering — glyphs appear as bright white forms against a very dark background. This appears to be either a photograph of a rubbing or high-contrast photographic treatment of the carved surface, different from the greyscale photograph used for Tablet B.

**Inscription density:** High — noticeably denser than Tablet B. Glyphs appear smaller relative to the surface and more closely packed per row.

**Row count (visible):** Approximately 12–13 horizontal rows can be counted.

**Boustrophedon:** Clearly present. The alternating orientation of rows is visible even at this scale.

**Glyph categories visually apparent:**
- Anthropomorphic figures present
- Rounded/oval glyphs noticeably frequent — visually distinguishable from the more linear shapes common on Tablet B. These rounded forms are consistent with the lunar glyphs expected in the calendar section (crescent, full disk shapes)
- Cross-shaped glyphs present
- Some glyphs appear slightly larger in certain zones — possibly the calendar section (lines C6–C8) has differently sized glyphs, though this is difficult to confirm at image resolution

**Surface condition:** Good overall.

---

### Tablet C Verso — Mamari (reverse face)
**Physical shape:** Same tablet as C recto.

**Damage:** Significant fire/burn damage visible in the upper-left quadrant. The darkened area occupies roughly the upper-left third of the surface — showing as a large, irregular zone of deep black charring. The damage is not uniform: it intensifies toward the upper-left corner and gradually fades to the right and toward the lower portion.

**Preservation:** The lower-right approximately two-thirds of the surface are intact and legible. The rows in the undamaged zone show similar glyph character and density to the recto.

**Implication for completeness:** The charring represents real information loss. Given the burn pattern — concentrated upper-left — the tablet was likely stored upright (or at an angle with the upper-left corner most exposed) when subjected to heat. This is consistent with the documented burning of Rapa Nui cultural material following the 1862 Peruvian slave raid and subsequent Christian conversion, which saw deliberate destruction of many tablets.

**Boustrophedon:** Visible in the intact rows of the lower section.

---

### Tablet D Side A — Échancrée
**Physical shape:** Substantially different from B and C. More rectangular overall, but with a notably irregular upper edge — broken or indented in what is described as the "échancrée" (notched) characteristic. The right-hand edge also shows irregular/broken areas. The tablet appears to have suffered physical damage to its edges at some point.

**Photographic technique:** Lighter overall treatment than C recto or D side B — glyphs appear as darker marks on a lighter grey background. This is the inverse contrast convention from C recto.

**Inscription density:** Lower than both B and C. Glyphs are visibly larger and more spaced out.

**Row count (visible):** The intact lower portion shows approximately 6–7 rows. The upper area with irregular edges shows only partial rows or traces, confirming physical damage affected inscription completeness.

**Glyph character:** The larger glyph size is observable — individual signs are more easily distinguished at this image resolution than on B or C.

**Scribal quality:** The inscription appears precise and well-formed in the intact lower rows — consistent with the secondpass documentation describing side A as "expertly carved."

---

### Tablet D Side B — Échancrée (reverse face)
**Physical shape:** Strikingly different visual impression from side A. The tablet appears elongated and curved — a crescent or canoe-like silhouette, narrowing at both ends. This is the same physical tablet as side A but photographed from the opposite face, revealing the full three-dimensional shape of the échancrée tablet: wedge-shaped, with one broad side and one narrower, curved side.

**Photographic technique:** Very high contrast — bright white glyphs on very dark background, similar to C recto. This is the clearest, highest-contrast photograph of any tablet in the imaged set.

**Inscription density:** Moderate-high. Glyphs are larger than on C but denser than on D side A.

**Row count (visible):** Approximately 8 rows clearly visible.

**Boustrophedon:** The clearest demonstration of boustrophedon alternation across all 5 images. Individual glyphs in adjacent rows can be seen to be clearly mirrored/rotated in orientation relative to each other.

**Scribal quality:** Noticeably different from side A — glyphs are larger and less refined, consistent with the secondpass documentation describing side B as "larger, cruder glyphs" from "a different scribe."

## Cross-Tablet Structural Observations

The following patterns emerge from comparing all 5 faces without imposing any interpretation:

### 1. Boustrophedon is universal and consistent
All 5 images show clear alternating row orientation. This is not a peculiarity of any one tablet — it is a hard structural constant of the script. The clearest demonstration is on D side B.

### 2. Glyph density varies significantly by tablet (and by scribal hand)
Ranked highest to lowest density:
- C recto: highest (~12–13 rows, small dense glyphs)
- B recto: moderate (~11 rows, medium spacing)
- D side B: moderate-high (~8 rows, larger glyphs)
- D side A: lower (~6–7 rows intact, larger, more spaced glyphs)

This variation is real and raises a genuine research question: does lower density correlate with a different genre, a different period, a different scribal tradition, or simply a larger original tablet with more available space?

### 3. Two distinct photographic conventions are present
Images alternately show dark-background/white-glyph (C recto, D side B) and light-background/dark-glyph (B recto, C verso, D side A) treatments. This is important for research methodology — glyph identification from photographs is affected by which convention was used, and results from one type should not be directly compared with the other without normalisation.

### 4. The oval/rounded glyph cluster is visually denser on C recto than on B or D
On Tablet C recto, rounded oval forms are noticeably more frequent relative to the other shape categories than on Tablet B or D. This is consistent with — though does not prove — the concentration of lunar calendar glyphs (crescent forms, full-disk forms) in Tablet C's well-documented calendar section.

### 5. The two sides of Tablet D were written by different scribes — visually confirmable
Side A: smaller, more precisely carved glyphs. Side B: larger, less refined glyphs. This is directly observable in the photographs and confirmed by the secondpass documentation. The implication — that the two sides contain independent texts written at different times — is a structural conclusion, not an interpretation.

### 6. Tablet C verso damage is localised and directional
The burn damage to C verso is concentrated upper-left and fades toward lower-right. This directionality is observable fact. Combined with the intact lower-right portion, we can say with confidence that the recto is fully intact and that C verso preserves roughly two-thirds of its original content.

---

## What This Phase Establishes for All Subsequent Phases

1. Any claim about cross-tablet glyph patterns must account for the **different photographic conventions** used — B recto and D side B are not comparable at face value without normalisation.
2. **C verso is partially lost** — any analysis counting Mamari's total content must acknowledge the ~30–35% loss to the upper-left burn.
3. **Tablet D's two sides are independent texts** — they should be analysed separately before any attempt is made to synthesise them.
4. The **rounded/oval glyph frequency difference** between C recto and B/D is the most immediately interpretable cross-tablet visual signal — it points directly to the calendar content concentration.
5. **Row counts and density estimates** from this phase will be referenced in all subsequent visual analyses rather than re-observed.
