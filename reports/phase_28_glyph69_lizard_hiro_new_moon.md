## The Three Meaning Clusters

### Cluster A: Literal Animal — Lizard/Gecko (*moko*)
The pictographic base is a lizard or gecko. *Moko* is the Eastern Polynesian word for lizard. Geckos were present on Rapa Nui and appear in local tradition. In mundane contexts, G69 = a lizard.

### Cluster B: Deity — Hiro (Rain God)
**Hiro** is a major figure in pan-Polynesian mythology:
- In Māori tradition: Hiro is the god of thieves and rain
- In Tahitian tradition: Hiri is a navigator and culture-hero
- In Rapa Nui tradition: Hiro is associated with the new moon and rain

The lizard's connection to Hiro likely comes from: lizards emerge after rain, lizards are associated with moisture and renewal, and Hiro as rain-bringer is naturally symbolised by a creature that appears when it rains.

### Cluster C: Calendar Role — New Moon Opener
The new moon is the **moment of beginning** — the lunar month starts here. Placing G69 (new moon / Hiro) at the very start of the Mamari calendar sequence is a structural choice:

> The month begins when Hiro appears → the lizard appears → rain comes → the cycle of growth begins

This is the **calendar as cosmology**: the new moon is not merely an astronomical event but Hiro's arrival, signalling rain and renewal.

## G69 vs G280: Opener vs Closer

A striking structural pair emerges in the Mamari calendar:

| | Glyph | Meaning | Position | Conf |

| **Calendar opener** | G69 | Lizard / Hiro / New Moon | First night of cycle | 0.85 |
| **Calendar closer** | G280 | Turtle / Rongo / Dark Moon | Last nights of cycle | 0.95 |

The month literally begins with the lizard and ends with the turtle. Hiro (rain, new beginnings) opens; Rongo (agriculture, waning, the deep ocean) closes. This is a **symbolic frame** for the entire lunar month: from Hiro's rain at new moon to Rongo's dark sea at month's end.

This pairing — lizard at start, turtle at end — is not a coincidence or an invented interpretive frame. Both assignments are directly confirmed in the lexicon notes with specific positional statements ("marks beginning of lunar cycle" for G69; "dark moon phase" for G280).

## The Mesoamerican Day-Sign Comparison (a Note of Caution)

The lexicon meanings include: *"comparable to serpents in Mesoamerican day-signs (e.g. 'Chikchan')"*

This comparison appears in the research corpus but should be treated carefully. The structural similarity (a reptile marking a calendar position) is real, but it does not imply historical contact between Mesoamerica and Rapa Nui. The parallel is a **typological coincidence** — reptile symbolism in calendar systems occurs independently across cultures because reptiles shed their skin (renewal), emerge seasonally, and are associated with rain in many tropical ecologies. The Rongorongo lizard is grounded in Polynesian tradition (Hiro, *moko*) — not in Mayan glyphics.
