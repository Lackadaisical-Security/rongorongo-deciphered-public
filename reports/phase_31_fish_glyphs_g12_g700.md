# Phase 31 — G700 Fish/Victim and G12 Fish/Divine Name: The Two Fish Glyphs

**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Status:** Complete (Evidence-anchored)

## Two Fish Glyphs — Why This Distinction Matters

The corpus has **two distinct fish glyphs** with different distributions and different dominant meanings:

| Glyph | Primary | Key polysemy | Conf | Occ | Tablets |
|-------|---------|-------------|------|-----|---------|
| **G12** | Fish (*ika*) / aquatic motion | Also: divine names (*Ika roa*) | 0.80 | 45 | Mamari, Santiago Staff |
| **G700** | Fish (*ika*) **or** victim/sacrifice | Also: *kai* (victim), *haka ira* (sacrifice) | 0.95 | 31 | Santiago Staff, Small Santiago |

They are not interchangeable. Their tablet distributions alone tell the story: G12 appears on Mamari (calendar, non-sacrifice context) while G700 appears on the Staff and Small Santiago (creation chant and genealogical/memorial contexts).

## G12: The Mamari Calendar Fish

### Lexicon Data
Meanings: "fish pictograph", "aquatic motion", "fish (*ika*)", "fish (verb)", "in divine names such as *Ika roa*"  
Transliterations: *ika, ika (as verb), rere vai*  
Contexts: aquatic, creation_mythology, marine_life  
Note: "Depicts creature in motion through water. May represent swimming action or specific marine animal in narrative."

### The Orientation-Flip in the Mamari Calendar
Barthel (1958) documented that certain glyphs in the Mamari calendar section **invert after the full moon (G152)**. G12 is one of these. The fish swims right-side-up in the waxing phase; after night 15 (Omotohi, full moon), it appears inverted — swimming the other way.

This is not damage or scribal error. It is an **intentional encoding convention**: the fish's direction represents the direction the moon is "moving" — growing toward full (fish pointing one way) or retreating toward dark (fish pointing the other way). The fish-flip is the most visually direct example of **orientation-as-meaning** in the entire script.

### G12 in Divine Names (*Ika roa*)
The notes mention G12 appears "in divine names such as *Ika roa*." *Ika roa* (literally "long fish") is a pan-Polynesian name for a star — the Milky Way or a specific constellation visible in the Pacific. On the Santiago Staff, G12 in a cosmogonic context would then read as this divine/celestial fish rather than a literal animal.

So G12's polysemy: literal fish → swimming motion → calendar direction marker → celestial divine name.

## G700: The Sacrifice Fish — kohau ika

### Lexicon Data
Meanings: "fish (*ika*) or victim/sacrifice (*haka ira*)", "victim (*kai*)", "sacrifice", "polysemic", "status: ambiguous"  
Transliterations: *ika, kai, haka ira*  
Contexts: historical, marine_life, mythological, sacrificial  
Note: "Fish pictograph that can mean fish or victim/sacrifice depending on context. Polysemic glyph."

### The *kohau ika* Connection
The traditional category of Rongorongo texts called **kohau ika** ("lines of the fish") are tablets recording casualties of war. The word *ika* in Rapanui has a confirmed dual meaning:
- *ika* = fish (literal)
- *ika* = a slain enemy, a war casualty, a sacrificial victim

This dual meaning is a documented Rapanui and wider Polynesian metaphor: enemies killed in battle are "caught like fish." The victors "fished" the battlefield. This is attested in oral tradition and ethnographic records (Métraux 1940, Englert 1948).

G700 encodes both meanings simultaneously. In a list context where G200 (chief) and personal name clusters appear before G700, the appropriate reading is:
> "Chief [Name] → [was killed / became a victim]"

In a creation chant context where G606 (birds) and G76 (copulate) precede G700:
> "...copulated with the fish [literal fish of the sea]"

The same glyph; the context determines whether you are reading a war memorial or a creation chant.

## The Scale of the Fish Metaphor

The Rapanui fish-as-victim metaphor is not a minor linguistic coincidence. It structured how war was understood:
- The battlefield was the ocean
- The warriors were fishermen
- The slain were the catch
- The *ika* list was the tally of fish brought home

This means the *kohau ika* tablets are not just casualty lists — they are structured as **fishing haul inventories**. The scribes deliberately chose G700 (fish/victim) to encode this, exploiting the polysemy as a culturally resonant framing device. Reading a war record as a fishing list was not ironic — it was the correct genre. Both are records of successful hunts.

## Key Findings

1. **Two distinct fish glyphs**: G12 (calendar, Mamari, divine names) and G700 (creation chant, memorial, sacrifice) — not interchangeable.
2. **G12 in the Mamari calendar** encodes direction (orientation flip = waning vs. waxing phase) — the most direct visual polysemy in the script.
3. **G700's fish/victim duality** is an intentional exploitation of the attested Rapanui linguistic double-meaning: *ika* = fish AND slain enemy.
4. **The *kohau ika* genre** of tablets is structured as a fishing haul — war death records intentionally framed as fish lists, using G700's polysemy as the genre marker.
5. G12 also appears in divine names (*Ika roa* — a celestial body) on the Santiago Staff — the fish reaches from calendar to cosmology to mythology.
