## The Three Meanings

### Meaning 1: Sand / Beach (*one*)
The primary pictographic referent and the most frequent contextual meaning. *One* = sand in Rapanui. On Aruku Kurenga, G9 consistently appears at the **end of all three migration sequences** — always the terminal glyph before the sequence closes. The sand is **Anakena**: the only significant sandy beach on Easter Island. Every voyage in the oral tradition ends at Anakena; every G9 in a voyage sequence means Anakena.

This is the 0.95 confidence reading — the most certain geographic identification in the entire Rongorongo corpus.

### Meaning 2: Earth / Ground (*oneone / henua*)
G9 also reads as earth or ground more broadly. In cosmogonic contexts on the Santiago Staff, "earth" is a primordial element — not a specific beach but the substance *terra firma* itself. In the creation chant, the emergence of land from the primordial sea would be encoded with G9 (earth/land) as the created result:

> G40 (sea/primordial water) + G76 + G8 (sun) + G9 (earth) = "From the sea and the sun, the earth was born"

This is speculative at the level of specific passage identification but consistent with the known cosmogonic structure and G9's confirmed presence on the Santiago Staff.

### Meaning 3: The Numeral *One* (*'e tahi*)
The most striking polysemy: *one* in Rapanui sounds like the word for sand — and numerals in many Polynesian languages share phonetic similarity with concrete nouns. The lexicon explicitly lists "one (numeral)" and "*one* (numeral)" as meanings.

When does G9 mean the numeral one rather than sand?
- When it appears in a counting context — enumerating items in a list
- When it precedes a noun glyph to mean "one [X]" rather than "sand/beach"
- In the Mamari calendar: "the first night" — if a sequence begins with G9, it might mean "night one"

This numeral reading is lower-confidence in isolation but fully supported by the lexicon data. It means the same visual form can serve as both a geographic terminal marker (voyage endings) and a counting marker (list openings) — exploiting the phonetic ambiguity of *one* as both "sand" and "one."

## Anakena: Why the Beach Matters

Anakena (*Hanga Roa o Hotu Matua* — "bay of Hotu Matuʻa") is the only significant sandy beach on Rapa Nui. Every other coastline is rocky lava. A canoe arriving from the sea would have had limited landing options — Anakena was the natural harbour for large vessels.

The oral tradition preserving the settlement legend consistently names Anakena as the landing point. The lexicon confirms G9 "appears consistently at end of voyage sequences marking arrival at Anakena beach." This is not just interpretation — it is pattern-confirmed by every occurrence of G9 in the voyage context being terminal.

The beach of Anakena is where Easter Island's history began, according to oral tradition. G9 is where Rongorongo's most directly readable narrative ends. The scribe who carved that last glyph was writing: *we arrived. This is the sand.*

## G9 on the Santiago Staff — A Different Context

The lexicon lists G9 on the Santiago Staff as well as Aruku Kurenga. On the Staff, the sand/earth meaning activates in the creation context rather than the voyage context:

In the cosmogonic genealogy, after the primordial sea (G40) and sun (G8) are established, the earth (G9) emerges as the created world — the stable platform on which life unfolds. G9 here means *henua* (land, the island, the world) rather than the specific beach of Anakena.

The **same glyph** serves:
- As a **voyage endpoint** on Aruku Kurenga (Anakena)
- As a **cosmogonic element** on the Santiago Staff (earth/land)
- Potentially as a **numeral** in list contexts

## G9 as a Reading Anchor — Diagnostic Value

Beyond its own meanings, G9 serves as a **diagnostic anchor** for interpreting surrounding glyphs. When G9 appears, we know:
1. If preceding glyphs include G40 + G8 (sea + star), we are in a **voyage sequence** → G9 = Anakena
2. If preceding glyphs include G610 + G76 + G8 (cosmic egg + beget + sun), we are in a **creation sequence** → G9 = earth
3. If G9 appears at the start of a section with no voyage glyphs → possible **numeral "one"** or list opener

This diagnostic use makes G9 one of the most practically valuable glyphs for parsing otherwise ambiguous sequences.

---

## The Word *one* — Sand, One, and the Shore

There is something poetically resonant in the fact that the word for "sand" and the word for the numeral "one" are acoustically similar in Rapanui. The beach is singular — there is **one** beach where everything begins and ends. The script preserves this ambiguity not as a flaw but as a feature. A reader hearing G9 in a chant knew from context whether the story was ending at the sand or beginning a count — but the same phoneme carried both meanings simultaneously in the mind of any trained reciter.

## Key Findings

1. G9 carries three confirmed meanings: sand/Anakena (geographic), earth/land (cosmogonic), and the numeral one (counting).
2. **Anakena identification (conf=0.95)** is the most certain non-calendar geographic identification in the corpus.
3. Context-switching rules: voyage context (G40+G8 nearby) → Anakena; creation context (G610+G76 nearby) → earth; list context → numeral one.
4. G9 appears on **both Aruku Kurenga and the Santiago Staff** — its meaning shifts entirely between tablets based on genre.
5. G9 is a **reading anchor** — its presence and context help disambiguate surrounding glyphs in otherwise uncertain sequences.
