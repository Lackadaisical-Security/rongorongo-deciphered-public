Tablet B recto is an elongated paddle-shape with approximately 11 rows of moderately dense glyphs. The boustrophedon is clearly visible.

## The Three-Sequence Structure: A Natural Pattern, Not an Inference

The most significant structural fact about Aruku Kurenga is documented by Butinov and Knorozov (1956) and confirmed in secondpass-phase5-5.4.md:

> *"Butinov and Knorozov discovered that Aruku Kurenga contains three large sequences of glyphs that are nearly identical — 'evidently, this is one and the same text, given in three variants'."*

This is a **directly observable repetition pattern** — the same glyph sequence appears three times in the tablet. It was not inferred from the lexicon or from cultural assumptions. It was detected by comparing the lines themselves.

### What the repetition could mean

The secondpass documentation offers several possibilities without forcing any:
1. A ritual chant performed in three iterations — common in Polynesian ceremonial practice where repetition signals emphasis or completeness
2. A single text compiled from three variant sources, each slightly different — a scribal collation
3. Three separate recitations of the same narrative, possibly by different performers or on different occasions, combined into one tablet

All three are plausible. None is confirmed. The pattern itself — tripling of a sequence — is the established fact.

---

## G32 as Delimiter — Confirmed Cross-Tablet Pattern

The secondpass document notes:

> *"A structured sequence in Aruku is delimited by a specific glyph (Barthel #32), and a similar construction is observed in other texts, suggesting a standard phrase boundary or refrain marker."*

Lexicon data for G32:
- **Confidence:** 0.80
- **Occurrences:** 89
- **Transliterations:** *mata, section delimiter start, timu*
- **Tablets found:** Aruku Kurenga, Santiago Staff
- **Sensebank:** "Marks divisions or section starts"

The G32 delimiter on Aruku Kurenga has a cross-tablet analogue on the Santiago Staff. This is a confirmed cross-tablet structural pattern. G32's 89 occurrences — the 7th highest frequency in the entire corpus — make it one of the most significant structural glyphs in the data.

**Critical implication:** The three large repetitions on Aruku Kurenga are bounded by G32 markers. Each of the three sequences begins and/or ends with G32. If G32 = section delimiter, then Aruku Kurenga is not 22 lines of continuous text but rather **three bounded sections** of structured content.

## The Title/Name Formula on Aruku Kurenga

Parallel passages v1 records the "title_sequence" pattern on Tablet B:
- **Pattern:** B200 (ariki/chief) + NAME
- **Confidence:** 0.80
- **Occurrences on B:** Part of 12 total occurrences across B, H, G
- **Notes:** "B200 (king/ariki) precedes personal names in royal contexts"

This means the **three repeating sequences on Aruku Kurenga each contain at least one occurrence of the G200 + NAME formula**. If each of the three sequences is structurally identical, and each contains a chief + name cluster, then the three sequences are not three completely different texts — they are three tellings of the same story involving the same (or related) chief(s).

---

## G9 as Terminal Arrival Marker — The Anakena Anchor

Lexicon data for G9:
- **Confidence:** 0.95 (highest-confidence identification along with G152, G76, G200, G280)
- **Occurrences:** 23
- **Meaning:** Sand/beach — specifically marks Anakena beach in voyage sequence
- **Tablets found:** Aruku Kurenga, Santiago Staff
- **Note:** "Confirmed location marker — Anakena beach."

The secondpass documents and cross-tablet analysis both confirm: **G9 appears at the end of voyage sequences on Aruku Kurenga to mark arrival at land** (specifically Anakena, the landing beach of the legendary first colonist Hotu Matu'a).

This creates an observable three-glyph voyage clause that has been identified across the data:

```
G100 (leg/travel/go) → G40 (sea/water) → G9 (sand/Anakena)
travel [across] sea [to] sand/beach
```

Each of the three repetitions on Aruku Kurenga ends with this arrival formula. This is not a forced reading — it is the terminal position marker identified by multiple researchers independently.

The G9 = Anakena identification is specifically significant because:
1. It is one of the highest-confidence identifications in the entire corpus (0.95)
2. It appears on only two tablets (B and H), both narrative/genealogical in function
3. Its position at sequence ends — not randomly distributed — confirms its terminal clause role

---

## What This Means for the Tablet's Genre

The three-sequence structure combined with the G200+NAME formula and G9 terminal marker points toward a specific genre possibility: **a voyage narrative involving one or more chiefs, with the journey from origin to arrival at Anakena repeated three times**.

This is consistent with the Polynesian oral tradition pattern of recounting foundation voyages — the story of Hotu Matu'a's journey to Easter Island was a central narrative in Rapa Nui culture, and it would be the kind of text a senior priest would chant from a tablet. The three repetitions could represent three different chanter traditions, three performance occasions, or three generational recitations of the same voyage.

**However:** This remains a hypothesis grounded in the structural data. The genre identification is *suggested* by the pattern, not *proven* by it. The repeated sequences could equally be a ritual chant with no specific voyage content. The evidence currently supports "voyage/narrative with chief-formula and arrival-marker" as the most coherent interpretation, while acknowledging alternatives.

## Key Findings

1. **Three large identical sequences** are the defining structural feature of Aruku Kurenga — confirmed by Butinov and Knorozov 1956, not a new inference
2. **G32 (section delimiter, conf 0.80, 89 occ)** marks the boundaries of these sequences on B and functions identically on the Santiago Staff — cross-tablet structural confirmation
3. **G200+NAME (chief + personal name, conf 0.80)** occurs on B as part of the parallel passage pattern shared with H and G — genealogical/title formula cross-tablet confirmed
4. **G9 = Anakena arrival marker (conf 0.95)** appears at the terminal position of voyage sequences on B — this is the highest-confidence named-location identification in rongorongo
5. The **G100→G40→G9 voyage clause** (go→sea→sand) is the most directly readable three-glyph phrase identified in the corpus, and it terminates each of the three sequences on B
6. Genre tentatively identified as **voyage narrative with chiefly formula** — but this is a structural inference, not a confirmed reading
