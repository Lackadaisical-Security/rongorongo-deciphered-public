# Phase 25 — Glyph 40: Water / Sea / Counter — Resolving the Polysemy

**Date:** 2026-03-08  
**Status:** Complete (Evidence-anchored)  
**Goal:** Resolve the apparent contradiction between G40 = "water/sea" (lexicon, conf 0.80) and earlier research documents describing G40 as a "night-count tally" in the Mamari calendar, by examining the full meaning array in the master lexicon.

---

## The Apparent Contradiction

In Phase 21 we flagged this problem:

- **Master lexicon:** G40 = "wavy line glyph representing water or sea" — transliterations: *vai, tai, rere* — contexts: fluid, marine, natural_elements, temporal_marker
- **Some research documents (rongorongo51.md et al.):** G40 = "a small vertical stroke" functioning as "night count marker" for Kokore nights in the Mamari calendar

These seem incompatible. A wavy water line and a small vertical stroke are visually distinct glyphs. *But is there a polysemic resolution?*

---

## The Full Meaning Array for G40 (from master lexicon)

The master lexicon lists **22 meanings** for G40. Beyond "water/sea/flow," the array explicitly includes:

> **"counter / ditto / enumerator"**  
> **"ditto"**  
> **"counter"**  

And the notes state:

> *"Wavy liquid representation: 'water' (vai) general liquid, 'sea' (tai) marine water, 'flow' (rere) movement. **Also functions as universal pictograph used in myths and voyages.**"*

The context tags include: `temporal_marker` — not just `marine`.

---

## Resolution: Polysemy, Not Contradiction

G40 is **genuinely polysemous** across three context domains:

### Domain 1: Literal Water/Sea (marine, voyage contexts)
- In Aruku Kurenga: G40 = sea crossing — the water that the canoe travels across
- In Santiago Staff: G40 = ocean/sea in cosmogonic contexts (the primordial waters)
- Translit: *vai* (fresh water) or *tai* (salt water/sea)

### Domain 2: Flow / Movement / Passage (temporal/cyclical)
- In any context involving passage of time or repeated occurrence: G40's wavy form encodes *rere* (flow, movement) — things that pass continuously
- In calendar context: the passing of nights "flows" like water — this is a documented Polynesian metaphor (time as a river, lunar cycles as tides)
- The `temporal_marker` context tag in the lexicon directly supports this

### Domain 3: Counter / Ditto / Enumerator (structural/grammatical)
- When repeated: G40 × N = N iterations of the same concept
- In the Mamari calendar: repeated G40s could mean "another [night], another [night]..." using the flow/repetition meaning
- This is how a wave works visually — each repetition is like another wave — making the counter meaning iconographically motivated

---

## The Kokore Nights: A Proposed Reconciliation

The Rapa Nui "Kokore" nights are **unnamed** (the word *kokore* literally means "nameless, lacking"). They appear as a series of nights with no individual names — just "first unnamed, second unnamed, third unnamed…"

If G40 appears **repeated in sequence** in the Mamari calendar (multiple consecutive G40s), this is coherently read as:
- **Water/flow reading:** the nights "flow past" unnamed like water
- **Counter/ditto reading:** "ditto, ditto, ditto" = "another one, another one…"

Neither meaning is wrong — they are facets of the same glyph used in a **context that exploits its water-as-flow/repetition sense** rather than its literal ocean sense.

The "vertical stroke" description in some research documents may be a misidentification or may refer to a specific reduced variant of G40. Without a full token-level transcription of the Mamari calendar section, this cannot be resolved definitively — but the polysemic path is now clear.

---

## G40 in Aruku Kurenga: Sea Crossing

On Tablet B (Aruku Kurenga), G40 appears in **voyage sequences** between the leader-glyph and the arrival-at-Anakena (G9). Here the literal meaning is primary: **sea/ocean** — the water crossed by the canoe. This is the most concrete, unambiguous use of G40.

The co-occurrence with G8 (sun/star — navigation) and G9 (sand — landfall) forms a three-part **navigation phrase**:
> G40 (sea) → G8 (star / bearing) → G9 (sand / arrival)

Each glyph contributes its primary literal meaning in the voyage context — water, stars, sand. This is the most direct reading in the corpus.

---

## G40 in the Santiago Staff: Primordial Waters

In the creation chant sections of the Staff, G40 likely represents the **primordial ocean** — the waters that existed before the world was ordered. This is a standard Polynesian cosmogonic element: *Te Kore* (the void) is often described as an undifferentiated sea. G40 in this context draws on both its water meaning and its temporal-flow meaning simultaneously — the primordial *before-time* waters.

---

## Key Findings

1. The lexicon **explicitly lists "counter/ditto/enumerator"** as meanings for G40 — the Mamari calendar use as a repeated-night marker is not a contradiction but an extension of G40's polysemic range.
2. The resolution is **iconographically motivated**: a wave/flow shape naturally encodes repetition and passage, not just literal water.
3. G40's three context domains (literal sea, temporal flow, enumerator) are all consistent with its wavy visual form — one shape, three related concepts.
4. In Aruku Kurenga: primarily sea/ocean (voyage context).
5. In Mamari calendar: primarily flow/repetition (temporal context) — supporting the Kokore-night interpretation but through polysemy, not misidentification.
6. In Santiago Staff: primordial waters (cosmogonic context).
